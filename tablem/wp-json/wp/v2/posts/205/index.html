Julia Child was a childhood icon of mine\u2014I used to watch her cooking show “The French Chef” religiously when I was young, which some would say was a harbinger of things to come. After living many years in Cambridge, Massachusetts, she moved to Santa Barbara, California. She loved to frequent a small taco stand called La Super-Rica Taqueria just a bit further down Highway 101 from Santa Barbara. We’d often stop at Super-Rica on trips between San Diego and San Luis Obispo.<\/span><\/p>\n\n\n\n

Horchata<\/h3>
  • Servings: <\/strong>4<\/li>
  • Print<\/a><\/li><\/ul>
    <\/p>\n\n\n

    When eating Mexican food, I sometimes find it hard to choose between a cold beer and a creamy, icy horchata to wash down a plate of carnitas, but at Super-Rica, the horchata wins hands down. This is our attempt to replicate it.<\/p>\n\n\n

    Ingredients<\/h4><\/p>\n\n\n\n
    • 1 c. long grain rice<\/li>
    • 4 c. whole milk<\/li>
    • \u00bd c. sugar<\/li>
    • 1 tsp. vanilla extract or paste (you can buy the vanilla paste at Trader Joe’s, or use the seeds of a vanilla bean and let the bean soak overnight)<\/li>
    • 1 cinnamon stick, broken into large pieces (Mexican cinnamon, not Chinese–it’s the brittle kind)<\/li><\/ul>\n\n\n\n

      <\/div><\/p>\n\n\n\n

      Directions<\/h4><\/p>\n\n\n\n
      1. Place the rice in a bowl with enough hot water to cover by 1 inch. Let the rice sit overnight.<\/li>
      2. Next day, pour off the water and reserve the water. Add enough fresh water to the reserved water to make 1 cup.<\/li>
      3. Place \u00bd cup of the rice water, \u00bd the soaked rice, and 2 cups milk in a blender. Blend until rice is all ground up. Do the same with the other half of the ingredients. Strain through 1 layer of cheesecloth.<\/li>
      4. If it seems too thick for your taste, strain through 2 or 3 layers of cheesecloth, as desired. Add sugar, vanilla, and cinnamon stick. It is better if left overnight again to \u201cimprove,\u201d as it gets richer in flavor. Serve over ice.<\/li><\/ol>\n\n\n\n
